Q: What is a sensible way to plan a small vegetable garden?
A: If you're starting small, don't try to conquer the world in one weekend. The most sensible way to do it is to work from the ground up—literally. First, look at your light. You need a spot that gets at least six to eight hours of direct sun. If you plant tomatoes in the shade, you aren't gardening; you're just growing sad, leggy stalks. Second, check your soil and water access. You want a place that is easy to reach with a hose. There is nothing more soul-crushing than dragging a heavy hose fifty feet every morning only to realize you forgot the zucchini. If your soil is heavy clay or pure sand, fix it with compost before you plant a single seed. Third, keep the variety small and the scale even smaller. Pick three or four things you actually like eating—maybe some basil, some cherry tomatoes, and some radishes—and start in a single raised bed or a few well-managed containers. It is much better to have a tiny, thriving garden than a massive, weed-choked disaster that makes you want to quit by July. Lastly, map it out. Draw a little sketch of where the sun hits and where the water reaches. Plan for airflow so you don't end up with a fungus party. Stick to the basics, observe how the space works, and let the garden tell you what it needs.
